2.4 Discussion

2.4.1 Biological and Competitive Rationale

Why Do Flat Horses Peak at Age 3?

The early peak in flat racing reflects physiological optimization for speed:

  • Physical maturity: Thoroughbreds achieve ~95% of adult bone density and muscle mass by age 3, with optimal power-to-weight ratio
  • Speed characteristics: Fast-twitch muscle fibers peak during ages 3-4, providing explosive speed
  • Racing structure: Prestigious flat races (Derby, Oaks, Guineas) are restricted to 3-year-olds

Post-peak decline occurs due to accumulated wear from high-speed racing, reduced metabolic efficiency, and retirement of top performers to breeding.

Why Do Hurdle Horses Peak at Age 6?

The later peak in hurdle racing emphasizes skill and experience:

  • Technical skills: Efficient jumping requires learned motor patterns developed over multiple races
  • Experience advantage: Horses develop better obstacle assessment, stride adjustment, and tactical awareness
  • Physical durability: Older horses have stronger bone/tendon structures to withstand jumping forces

Extended competitive window: Hurdle horses maintain performance longer (ages 8-10+) because jumping emphasizes stamina over pure speed, which degrades more slowly with age. Veteran jumpers compensate for reduced speed with superior technique and efficiency.

2.4.3 Limitations and Caveats

Limitations and Caveats

1. Sample Size Imbalances: Some age groups have very few horses (e.g., 599 Chase horses at age 4), creating unreliable estimates and potentially misleading patterns that require statistical adjustment methods.

2. Survivorship Bias: Our dataset only includes horses that survived training and continued racing, meaning older horses represent only the most durable and successful ones, making actual age-related decline appear less severe than reality.

3. Unmeasured Confounders: Age correlates with race quality, training intensity, and jockey quality, preventing definitive claims that age alone causes performance changes without considering these interrelated factors.

4. Race Distance Variations: Different distances within race types may have different peak ages (sprints favor younger horses, staying races favor older), which our aggregated analysis may miss.

5. Time Period Limitations: Our 2015-2022 data may not reflect changes in modern training methods, breeding practices, or economic factors, requiring validation with other time periods to confirm stability.

3.1 Rating Methodology

Conceptual Framework

Rating systems in horse racing serve to:

  1. Quantify historical performance in a standardized metric
  2. Enable fair comparisons across entities with varying experience levels
  3. Capture multiple performance dimensions (consistency, recent form, class)
  4. Provide predictive signals for future performance

Core Design Principles

1. Temporal Integrity (No Data Leakage)

Implementation:

  • Sequential processing: races sorted chronologically
  • For race on date D, ratings calculated using only races where date < D
  • Each race result added to history AFTER being used for prediction

Why Critical: Data leakage (using future information) creates artificially inflated model performance that fails in real-world application. This is the most common fatal flaw in sports analytics models.

2. Multi-Dimensional Performance Metrics

3. Handling Cold Start Problem

Challenge: New horses, jockeys, or trainers lack historical data for rating calculations.

Median Imputation: New entities are assigned the median rating of experienced entities in the same category. This approach assumes unknowns are likely average performers rather than poor ones, reduces prediction variance, and follows standard practice in collaborative filtering systems.

Rationale: We chose median imputation as it is more statistically robust than zero initialization and better represents the realistic expectation that most new entities will perform at average levels rather than at extremes.

4. Predictive Modeling -Q3

Overview

We developed machine learning models to predict race winners, generating calibrated win probabilities for each runner. The objective is to transform historical ratings and race features into actionable predictions that can identify value in betting markets.

Core Challenge: Horse racing is inherently stochastic - even the best horses lose frequently. Our goal is to quantify uncertainty and generate well-calibrated probabilities, not deterministic predictions.

4.1 Model Architecture

Modeling Philosophy

Problem Framing: Multi-Class Classification

Each race as independent prediction problem:

  • Input: Features for all runners in race
  • Output: Win probability for each runner
  • Constraint: Probabilities sum to 1 per race

Alternative framings considered:

Approach Description Advantages Disadvantages Decision
Binary Classification Predict win/loss per horse independently Simple, standard ML Ignores race structure; probabilities don't sum to 1 Rejected
Learning-to-Rank Rank all horses by finishing order Models full race order Complex, specialized algorithms needed Rejected (overkill)
Multinomial Classification Predict probability distribution over all runners Probabilities sum to 1; interpretable Requires calibration Selected

Rationale: Multinomial approach best balances mathematical correctness (probabilities constrained) with practical simplicity.

Model Selection Strategy

Ensemble Comparison Approach: Train multiple models, select best performer:

Models Evaluated

Model Selection Strategy

Approach: Train multiple models and compare performance based on ROC-AUC to select the best performer.

Model Type Rationale Expected ROC-AUC
Logistic Regression Linear, probabilistic Industry baseline; interpretable coefficients; fast & robust 0.70-0.75
Random Forest Ensemble (bagging) Captures interactions; handles mixed data; robust to outliers 0.75-0.80
Gradient Boosting Ensemble (boosting) State-of-art for tabular data; Kaggle-proven; captures non-linearities 0.78-0.82

Selection Criterion: Highest ROC-AUC on time-based test set

Ensemble Comparison Approach: After training all three models, we evaluate their individual performance and combine them using ensemble methods to leverage their complementary strengths.

Why These Models (Not Deep Learning)?

Deep neural networks were not used because:

Limited Data: Average horse has only 11 racesinsufficient for deep learning, which requires thousands of examples per entity. Tabular Data Advantage: Gradient boosting consistently outperforms deep learning on structured tabular data (proven in Kaggle competitions). Interpretability: Tree-based models provide clear feature importance, while neural networks are black boxes. Efficiency: GBMs train in minutes vs. hours for neural networks. Note: Deep learning could be reconsidered if dataset were 10x larger.

4.2 Feature Engineering

Feature Categories

1. Rating Features (From Q2)

Core predictors:

- horse_rating_scaled      # Horse quality (0-100)
- jockey_rating_scaled     # Jockey skill (0-100)
- trainer_rating_scaled    # Stable quality (0-100)
- combined_rating          # Weighted ensemble (0-100)

Why combined rating?

combined_rating = 0.5  horse + 0.3  jockey + 0.2  trainer

Rationale for weights:

  • Horse quality dominates (50%): "The horse makes the jockey" (industry saying)
  • Jockey skill matters (30%): Tactical decisions, riding style
  • Trainer contribution (20%): Preparation, race selection

Alternative: Let model learn optimal weights Rejected: Risk of overfitting with limited data Domain-informed weights more stable

2. Age Features

Raw age + non-linear term:

- age           # Linear effect
- age_squared   # Captures inverted-U shape (from Q1 analysis)

Why age? From Q1, we observed: Young horses: Improving with age, Peak age: Best performance, Old horses: Declining with age

Linear age: Assumes monotonic relationship (wrong) Polynomial age: Captures peak-then-decline (correct)

Mathematical justification:

Performance =  + (age) + (age)

If  < 0: Inverted parabola (peak exists)
Peak age = - / (2)

Why not age or higher? Additional complexity rarely justified, Age sufficient for inverted-U shape, Higher polynomials risk overfitting

3. Class/Ability Features

Handicapper ratings + weight:

- official_rating_filled   # BHA handicapper assessment
- carried_weight          # Weight horse must carry
- weight_relative         # Weight vs field average

official_rating_filled:

  • Official handicapper rating (26% missing, filled with race-type median)
  • Independent measure of horse ability
  • Complements our ratings (different methodology)

Why include despite having our ratings?

  • Captures information we don't: beaten distances, weight adjustments
  • Handicappers have decades of domain expertise
  • Empirical testing: including improves ROC-AUC by ~0.02

weight_relative:

weight_relative = horse_weight - mean(all_weights_in_race)

Why relative (not absolute)?

  • Absolute weight meaningless (varies by race)
  • Relative weight indicates advantage/disadvantage
  • Negative = lighter than average = advantage

4. Experience Features

Racing history indicators:

- horse_rating_runs              # Career starts (proxy for experience)
- horse_rating_days_since_last  # Freshness/layoff
- freshness_score               # Optimal freshness indicator

freshness_score logic:

if 7 <= days_since_last <= 28:
    freshness_score = 1.0      # Optimal freshness
elif days_since_last < 7:
    freshness_score = 0.5      # Too soon (not recovered)
elif days_since_last > 56:
    freshness_score = 0.5      # Too long (lost fitness)
else:
    freshness_score = 0.8      # Acceptable

Domain knowledge basis:

  • 7-28 days: Industry standard "ideal gap"
  • < 7 days: Risk of not fully recovered (especially jumps)
  • > 56 days: Fitness concerns, returning from injury/layoff

From Q2 rating components:

- horse_rating_form_last5     # Win rate in last 5 races
- jockey_rating_form_last20   # Jockey recent form
- trainer_rating_form_last30  # Stable recent form
- form_strength               # form  runs (confidence-weighted form)

form_strength:

form_strength = horse_rating_form_last5  horse_rating_runs

Rationale:

  • Form of experienced horse > form of novice
  • 2/5 wins with 50 career starts > 2/5 wins with 5 career starts
  • Weights form by reliability (experience)

Competition and conditions:

- race_distance              # Race length (meters)
- field_size / n_runners    # Number of competitors

avg_opponent_rating = mean(horse_rating_scaled for other_horses in race)

Why important?

  • Class of race indicator: High avg opponent rating = competitive race
  • Relative ability: Horse with 80 rating in:
    • Race with avg 70 = favorite
    • Race with avg 85 = underdog
  • Captures race difficulty not in other features

7. Market Information

Pre-race betting indicator:

- ltp_5min_filled    # Last traded price 5 minutes before start

Controversy: Should we include market information?

Arguments AGAINST:

  • Market already aggregates all public information (Efficient Market Hypothesis)
  • Including = just copying market, not adding value
  • Goal is to BEAT market, not learn from it

Arguments FOR (our position):

Strategy 3:

Kelly Criterion

Rationale: - Optimal bet sizing (maximizes long-term growth), Bets more on high-edge, less on low-edge, Naturally weights by confidence

Approach:

For each positive edge:
    kelly_fraction = edge / (odds - 1)
    kelly_fraction = min(kelly_fraction, 0.10)  # Cap at 10% of bankroll
    stake = bankroll  kelly_fraction

Theory: Kelly Criterion maximizes expected log wealth:

f* = (bp - q) / b

Where:
f* = fraction of bankroll to bet
b = odds received (decimal odds - 1)
p = true probability (model estimate)
q = 1 - p

Example:

Horse at 5.0 odds (market), model says 30% win prob:
edge = 0.30 - 0.20 = 0.10 (10% edge)
kelly = 0.10 / (5.0 - 1) = 0.025 (2.5% of bankroll)

If bankroll = 100:
Bet = 2.50

Results:

Bets: 85,702 (all positive edges)
Total staked: 24,989.99 (dynamic sizing)
Total returned: 25,558.55
Profit/Loss: 568.55
ROI: +2.28%

 PROFITABLE

Why it succeeded:

The Kelly Criterion provides risk-weighted allocationbetting more on strong edges (5%+) and less on weak edges (1-2%), naturally concentrating capital on the best opportunities. The 10% cap prevents bankroll ruin while surviving variance, and the strategy is mathematically optimal for maximizing long-term growth rate. Despite placing 85,702 bets, only 24,990 was staked (versus 85,702 in flat betting), effectively concentrating 70% of capital on the top 30% of edgescombining volume with discipline.

Technical details:

We implemented fractional Kelly with a 10% cap to reduce variance at the cost of some growtha conservative approach compared to aggressive full Kelly. Starting with a 100 bankroll and reinvesting winnings, the strategy achieved +0.57% growth over 16 months, translating to an annualized return of ~0.43% per year. While very modest, this positive compounding return demonstrates the strategy can systematically extract value from the market without risking bankroll ruin.

5.3 Statistical Significance Testing

Hypothesis Testing Framework

Why statistical tests matter:

  • Observed profits could be luck (random variance)
  • Need to rule out chance with confidence
  • Standard: p < 0.05 (95% confidence)

Statistical Significance Tests

Test 1: Strategy 1 Profitability (One-Sample T-Test)

Hypothesis: Mean profit = 0 (break-even)

Results:

  • Mean profit per bet: -0.074
  • T-statistic: -3.268, P-value: 0.0011
  • STATISTICALLY SIGNIFICANT (highly unprofitable)

Interpretation: Only a 0.11% chance these losses occurred by random chance. The model systematically overestimates win probabilities when betting all positive edgesthis is a fundamental flaw, not bad luck.

Test 2: Value Bets vs Market (Binomial Test)

Hypothesis: Value bets win MORE than market predicts

Results:

  • Expected wins: 7,344 | Actual wins: 7,409 (+65 wins, +0.9%)
  • P-value: 0.2156
  • NOT statistically significant

Interpretation: The +65 extra wins (0.08% improvement) could easily occur by chance. The model's value identification is weaksmall edges are mostly noise. Stronger selection criteria (5%+ threshold) are needed to achieve statistical significance.

Test 3: Bootstrap Confidence Interval (Strategy 1)

Method: 1,000 resamples with replacement

Results:

  • 95% CI for ROI: [-11.84%, -2.80%]
  • Upper bound < 0 (even best case loses money)

Interpretation: We're 95% confident the true ROI lies between -11.84% and -2.80%. Break-even (0%) is not in the intervalrobust evidence of unprofitability across all plausible scenarios, not just a point estimate.

Statistical Significance Summary

Three tests, consistent message:

  1. T-test: Strategy 1 losses are statistically significant (p < 0.05)
  2. Binomial test: Value bets don't significantly beat market expectations
  3. Bootstrap CI: Strategy 1 unprofitable even in best-case scenario

  • Model's value identification is weak (small edge noise)

5.5 Discussion

Why Model Achieves Only Marginal Profitability

Market Sophistication: Betfair attracts elite participants including professional syndicates, algorithmic trading firms, and racing insiders with private information. Our model relies solely on public data and represents a single-person academic project competing against teams of PhD quants with six-figure bankrolls. In this context, achieving even a small positive edge is noteworthylarger profits would likely require proprietary data sources, real-time market monitoring, and faster execution capabilities we don't possess.

Information Incorporation Speed: Market odds update continuously as new information emerges, while our model generates static predictions. By the time we record ltp_5min (5 minutes before the race), prices already reflect stable rumors, track observations, and late informed money. Our model is trained on post-information prices but must make pre-information predictionswe're inherently behind the information curve. The market's ability to instantly incorporate breaking news (e.g., trainer comments, track conditions) gives it a persistent advantage over historical pattern-based predictions.

Favorite-Longshot Bias: While classic racing markets exhibit exploitable biases (favorites overbet, longshots underbet), our model doesn't systematically exploit this. Heavy reliance on ltp_5min means our predictions tend to agree with market favorites rather than identifying undervalued longshots. This limits our ability to capture one of the few known persistent inefficiencies in betting markets, constraining profitability to marginal levels.
